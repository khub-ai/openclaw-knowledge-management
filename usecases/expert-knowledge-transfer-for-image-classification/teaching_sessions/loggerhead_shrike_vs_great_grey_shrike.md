# Loggerhead Shrike vs Great Grey Shrike: Identification Guide

**Source**: All About Birds (Cornell Lab of Ornithology); Sibley Guide to Birds; BWP
**Feature group**: facial mask + rump + size
**Attribute similarity (CUB-200-2011)**: 0.9776

## Overview
Both are grey, black, and white predatory songbirds with hooked bills and black face masks. The Great Grey Shrike is the larger, Old World counterpart. In North America, where range overlaps only marginally in the far north, size and mask details are the primary separators. In the CUB-200-2011 dataset, both species appear and must be distinguished on visual features alone.

## Key Identification Features

### 1. Size
Great Grey Shrike is noticeably larger — approximately Robin-to-Starling sized versus Loggerhead's Robin-sized but slimmer body. Great Grey appears longer-tailed, heavier-bodied, and longer-billed. When perched, the size difference is apparent if any size reference is available.

### 2. Facial mask extent
Loggerhead Shrike's black mask is broader across the forehead and extends across the base of the upper bill, meeting above the bill. Great Grey Shrike's mask is narrower across the forehead and does not extend across the base of the upper mandible — the forehead above the bill is pale grey or white, giving the face a more open expression. This is one of the most reliable visual marks.

### 3. Rump color
Great Grey Shrike shows a white rump patch that is conspicuous in flight. Loggerhead Shrike's rump is grey, blending with the back. The white rump on Great Grey is especially visible as the bird flies away.

### 4. Underpart pattern
Great Grey Shrike has fine grey barring (vermiculations) on the underparts, particularly the breast and flanks. Loggerhead Shrike adults have white or very pale grey underparts with little or no visible barring. This difference is subtle but consistent.

### 5. Scapular / wing white
Both species show white in the wing, but Great Grey typically shows a broader white patch at the base of the primaries and a larger white scapular area. In flight, Great Grey's wings look more extensively white.

## In the Field: Decision Rules
- If the black mask extends across the forehead above the bill base, it is a Loggerhead Shrike.
- If the forehead above the bill base is pale/grey (mask does not cross above bill), lean toward Great Grey Shrike.
- If a white rump patch is visible as the bird flies, it is a Great Grey Shrike.
- If the bird is noticeably large and long-tailed, lean toward Great Grey Shrike.
- If the underparts show fine grey barring, it is a Great Grey Shrike.
- If underparts are clean white with no barring, lean toward Loggerhead Shrike.

## Female / Immature / Non-breeding
Immature Loggerhead Shrikes show fine brown barring on the underparts, which can look similar to Great Grey. Key: check the mask — immature Loggerhead still shows the wider mask crossing the bill base. Immature Great Grey shows stronger barring.

## Voice
Both give harsh, mechanical chattering calls. Voice is not reliable for distinguishing the two species from images.

## Quick Reference
| Feature | Loggerhead Shrike | Great Grey Shrike |
|---|---|---|
| Size | Medium (9 in) | Large (9.5–10.5 in) |
| Mask across bill base | Yes — broad mask | No — pale forehead |
| Rump | Grey | White (visible in flight) |
| Underparts | Clean white | Finely grey-barred |
| Tail | Medium | Longer |
| Range | N. America | Eurasia; rare N. America |
