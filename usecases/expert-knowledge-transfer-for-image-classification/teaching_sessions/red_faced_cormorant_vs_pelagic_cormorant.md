# Red-faced Cormorant vs Pelagic Cormorant: Identification Guide

**Source**: All About Birds (Cornell Lab of Ornithology); Sibley Guide to Birds
**Feature group**: facial skin + body size + bill color
**Attribute similarity (CUB-200-2011)**: 0.9623

## Overview
Both are dark, iridescent cormorants of the North Pacific coast, nesting on rocky cliffs and sea stacks. Pelagic Cormorant is the smallest and slenderest North American cormorant; Red-faced is medium-sized. Bare facial skin extent and color, and bill color, are the primary distinguishing features.

## Key Identification Features

### 1. Bare facial skin (most reliable)
Red-faced Cormorant has extensive, bright red-orange bare facial skin surrounding the eye and extending across the forehead and around the bill base — the "red face" is the eponymous feature and visible at moderate range. Pelagic Cormorant has much more restricted bare skin: a small reddish-pink patch at the base of the bill and gape only, with no red across the forehead. If the bare red skin extends broadly around the eye and onto the forehead, it is a Red-faced Cormorant.

### 2. Bill color
Red-faced Cormorant has a pale, yellowish to greyish-blue bill. Pelagic Cormorant has a dark bill throughout — dark grey to blackish. This is visible at close range.

### 3. Body size and proportions
Pelagic Cormorant is the slenderest cormorant — it has an exceptionally thin neck, tiny head, and slender body. It looks almost snake-like in the water. Red-faced Cormorant is larger and heavier-bodied, with a proportionally larger head and thicker neck. In flight, Pelagic appears very slim and dainty compared to other cormorants.

### 4. Iridescence
Both species are brilliantly iridescent in breeding plumage. Pelagic Cormorant has intense green and purple iridescence across the body and wings — arguably the most iridescent North American cormorant. Red-faced Cormorant is also iridescent but with a more bronzy-green quality and less intense purple sheen. This is a supporting feature that requires good light.

### 5. White flank patches (breeding)
Breeding Pelagic Cormorant develops conspicuous white flank patches on either side of the rump during the breeding season. Red-faced Cormorant does not show these white flank patches. This is seasonal and not present year-round.

## In the Field: Decision Rules
- If bright red-orange bare skin extends across the forehead and around the eye, it is a Red-faced Cormorant.
- If bare skin is restricted to a small area at the bill base only, it is a Pelagic Cormorant.
- If the bill is pale (yellowish or greyish), it is a Red-faced Cormorant.
- If the bill is dark throughout, lean toward Pelagic Cormorant.
- If the bird appears exceptionally slender with a tiny head and very thin neck, it is a Pelagic Cormorant.
- If white flank patches are visible on a dark cormorant in breeding season, it is a Pelagic Cormorant.

## Female / Immature / Non-breeding
Both sexes identical in both species. Immatures are dark brownish-black; the facial skin features are reduced in young birds. Pelagic Cormorant immatures retain the dainty proportions. Red-faced Cormorant immatures show some pale skin at the face but less red than adults.

## Voice
Both are largely silent away from breeding colonies; limited vocal differentiation useful from photographs.

## Quick Reference
| Feature | Red-faced Cormorant | Pelagic Cormorant |
|---|---|---|
| Facial skin | Extensive red across forehead + eye | Small reddish patch at bill base only |
| Bill | Pale yellowish-grey | Dark throughout |
| Size | Medium (28–31 in) | Small (25–28 in) |
| Neck | Moderate | Very thin, snake-like |
| Breeding white patches | None | White flank patches |
| Iridescence | Bronze-green | Brilliant green-purple |
