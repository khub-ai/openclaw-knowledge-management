# Indigo Bunting vs Blue Grosbeak: Identification Guide

**Source**: All About Birds (Cornell Lab of Ornithology); Sibley Guide to Birds
**Feature group**: size + bill + wingbars
**Attribute similarity (CUB-200-2011)**: 0.9719

## Overview
Both species are brilliant blue songbirds of open scrubby habitats in North America. Males are unmistakable within their own species, but when seen briefly or without size reference, they are frequently confused. Blue Grosbeak is nearly twice the mass of Indigo Bunting, and bill size and wingbars are the decisive marks.

## Key Identification Features

### 1. Bill size (most important)
Blue Grosbeak has a massive, deep, triangular bill — one of the largest bills relative to body size of any North American passerine. Indigo Bunting has a small, conical, seed-cracker bill appropriate to its finch-like size. Even in photos this difference is usually obvious: the Grosbeak's bill dominates the face; the Bunting's bill is a small point.

### 2. Body size
Blue Grosbeak is considerably larger — approximately the size of a House Sparrow or small starling (6.5–7.5 in, 26–31 g). Indigo Bunting is much smaller, roughly sparrow-sized (4.7–5.1 in, 12–18 g). When perched next to vegetation or a branch of known size, this difference is usually apparent.

### 3. Wingbars
Blue Grosbeak has two bold, chestnut-rufous wingbars on the closed wing — this is a consistent and reliable field mark. Indigo Bunting lacks bold wingbars; at most it shows faint, thin, bluish wingbars that are difficult to see at any distance.

### 4. Chestnut on wings
Blue Grosbeak males show extensive chestnut-rufous coloring on the wing coverts, not just as wingbars but as a substantial area of warm brown on the wing. Indigo Bunting is solid blue with no warm brown tones in the wing.

### 5. Head and body iridescence
Both males appear brilliant turquoise-blue in good light and dull or blackish in poor light. Grosbeak's blue tends toward deeper cobalt; Bunting's blue can appear more cyan or turquoise — but this varies with light and angle and should not be relied upon.

## In the Field: Decision Rules
- If the bill is massive and deep (as large as the bird's eye or larger), it is a Blue Grosbeak.
- If bold chestnut-rufous wingbars are present on the closed wing, it is a Blue Grosbeak.
- If the bill is small and pointed, it is an Indigo Bunting.
- If no wingbars or only very faint wingbars are present, lean toward Indigo Bunting.
- If the bird is noticeably larger than a sparrow, it is a Blue Grosbeak.

## Female / Immature / Non-breeding
Female Blue Grosbeak is rich warm brown with chestnut wingbars and a large bill — much warmer and brighter than female Indigo Bunting, which is pale brownish-streaked with a small bill and faint wingbars. The bill size difference is diagnostic for females as well. Immature males of both species show patchy blue and brown; the bill and wingbar features still apply.

## Voice
Indigo Bunting: complex, paired phrases sung high and sweet. Blue Grosbeak: rich, melodious warble with a buzzy quality. Voice is useful but requires experience.

## Quick Reference
| Feature | Indigo Bunting | Blue Grosbeak |
|---|---|---|
| Size | Small (4.7–5.1 in) | Medium (6.5–7.5 in) |
| Bill | Small, conical | Massive, deep triangular |
| Wingbars | Absent or faint | Bold chestnut-rufous |
| Wing pattern | Solid blue | Blue with chestnut coverts |
| Female | Streaked buff-brown | Rich brown with chestnut wingbars |
