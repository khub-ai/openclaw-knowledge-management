# Common Tern vs Forster's Tern: Identification Guide

**Source**: All About Birds (Cornell Lab of Ornithology); Sibley Guide to Birds
**Feature group**: tail pattern + wingtip + non-breeding head pattern
**Attribute similarity (CUB-200-2011)**: 0.9678

## Overview
Common and Forster's Terns are medium-sized terns with red-orange bills, black caps, and grey-white bodies. They breed in partially overlapping ranges in North America and are frequently confused. Wingtip pattern, tail color, and non-breeding head pattern are the most reliable separators.

## Key Identification Features

### 1. Wingtip pattern (best mark in flight)
Common Tern has dark grey outer primaries that create a distinct dark wedge or trailing edge on the upperwing. In worn summer plumage the dark tips become very contrasting. Forster's Tern has silvery-white outer primaries that are paler than the rest of the wing — the wingtip is frosted, not dark. In flight from above, if the wingtip is dark, it is a Common Tern; if the wingtip is pale silvery, it is a Forster's Tern.

### 2. Tail pattern
Common Tern has a grey tail with white outer edges — the tail is mostly grey when seen from above or below. Forster's Tern has a white tail with grey central feathers — the tail appears mostly white. Forster's tail is also longer and the outer feathers have grey outer webs giving a frosty appearance.

### 3. Non-breeding / winter head pattern (very reliable)
Non-breeding Common Tern shows a broad black bar from the eye to the nape, with a white forehead — similar to many terns. Non-breeding Forster's Tern shows a distinctive black patch restricted to the area around and behind the eye (like a black eye-patch or "Zorro mask"), with an otherwise white head. This winter head pattern is unique among North American terns and is diagnostic for Forster's.

### 4. Bill color and shape
Breeding Common Tern: orange-red bill with a black tip. Breeding Forster's Tern: orange bill with less black at the tip — often appearing all-orange. Non-breeding bills of both are darker; Forster's often retains more orange at the base.

### 5. Underparts
Breeding Common Tern often shows a grey wash on the breast and belly. Breeding Forster's Tern has clean white underparts. This is a supporting, not diagnostic, feature.

## In the Field: Decision Rules
- If the outer primaries are frosted/silvery (pale wingtip), it is a Forster's Tern.
- If the outer primaries form a dark wedge on the upperwing, it is a Common Tern.
- In non-breeding plumage: if the head shows a bold eye-patch (black around and behind the eye, white forehead), it is a Forster's Tern.
- If the tail is mostly grey from above, lean toward Common Tern.
- If the tail is mostly white (with grey central feathers only), lean toward Forster's Tern.
- If the breeding bill is orange-red with a distinct black tip, lean toward Common Tern.

## Female / Immature / Non-breeding
Juvenile Common Tern shows a dark carpal bar and scaly mantle, features reduced in juvenile Forster's. The wingtip and tail features still apply. Non-breeding Forster's eye-patch is the most useful feature for separating winter birds.

## Voice
Common Tern: drawn-out "keeeerrr" descending in pitch; also "kip-kip-kip." Forster's: nasal "kzrrr" or "kerr." Voice is not useful from photographs.

## Quick Reference
| Feature | Common Tern | Forster's Tern |
|---|---|---|
| Wingtip (upperwing) | Dark grey trailing edge | Frosted silver, pale |
| Tail color | Mostly grey | Mostly white |
| Non-breeding head | Black bar eye to nape | Black eye-patch only |
| Bill (breeding) | Orange-red, black tip | Orange, less black |
| Underparts | Grey-washed breast | Clean white |
| Tail length | Moderate | Longer outer feathers |
