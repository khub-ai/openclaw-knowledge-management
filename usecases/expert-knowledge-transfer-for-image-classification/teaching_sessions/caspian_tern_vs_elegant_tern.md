# Caspian Tern vs Elegant Tern: Identification Guide

**Source**: All About Birds (Cornell Lab of Ornithology); Audubon Field Guide; Sibley Guide to Birds
**Feature group**: Bill size/color/shape, body size, primary underside pattern, crest shape
**Attribute similarity (CUB-200-2011)**: ~0.69

## Overview
Both are large, crested terns with black caps and orange-to-red bills that can occur together along the Pacific Coast of North America and in parts of the Gulf Coast. At a distance, both look like "large terns with big orange bills," making them a frequent source of confusion. The key insight is that the Caspian Tern is massively larger — the largest tern in the world — with a thick blood-red bill and mostly dark wing undersides, while the Elegant Tern is noticeably smaller and more slender with a longer, drooping orange bill, long droopy crest, and pale wing undersides.

## Key Identification Features

### 1. Overall Size and Build — Most Immediately Apparent
Caspian Tern is enormous: 19–21 inches in length with a wingspan of 50–55 inches, comparable in bulk to a Ring-billed Gull. It has a large, heavy body with broad wings and a powerful, direct flight. Elegant Tern is substantially smaller at 15–17 inches with a wingspan of 34 inches — roughly comparable to a Royal Tern but more slender. When the two species are on the same beach or perched together, the size difference is immediately obvious. If the tern looks gull-sized, it is almost certainly Caspian; if it looks slim and medium-large, consider Elegant.

### 2. Bill Size, Color, and Shape
Caspian Tern has a very large, thick, blood-red to orange-red bill — the bill appears massive relative to the head, with no droop, straight and heavy-tipped. The tip may show a small dark subterminal smudge in some birds. The bill's sheer bulk is a field mark in itself. Elegant Tern has a noticeably different bill: long, slender, distinctly orange (warmer orange, less red), and with a characteristic drooping tip — the distal third of the bill curves or droops slightly downward, giving it a slightly hook-tipped appearance unlike the straight, blunt Caspian bill. If the bill droops at the tip → Elegant; if it is straight and massive → Caspian.

### 3. Underwing Pattern in Flight
This is the most reliable separator in flight. Caspian Tern shows dark gray to blackish outer primaries on the underwing — a large dark area covering most of the outer wing from below, creating a bold dark trailing wedge on the underwing. This dark underside to the primaries is the single most reliable flight mark for Caspian Tern. Elegant Tern has pale gray outer primaries from below, with only the tips showing dark; the underwing appears almost entirely white or very pale gray, with dark restricted to the feather tips. In flight: extensively dark underwing primaries → Caspian; pale underwing with only dark tips → Elegant.

### 4. Crest Length and Shape
Elegant Tern has a long, shaggy black crest that droops conspicuously from the back of the crown, sometimes long enough to trail behind the bird — in strong wind, the crest streams backward dramatically. This long, floppy crest is one of Elegant's most characteristic features. Caspian Tern has a much shorter, stiffer black crest — more like a short bristly cap or a crew-cut; it does not stream or droop conspicuously. A long, drooping or streaming crest → Elegant Tern.

### 5. Cap Pattern in Non-breeding Plumage
In non-breeding plumage (fall and winter), Caspian Tern retains a mostly complete black cap but with streaky white on the forehead — the cap appears dark and messy. Elegant Tern in non-breeding plumage develops a white forehead with a black mask behind the eye and a retained black rear crown crest, giving it a "masked bandit" appearance. Both species are easier to identify in breeding plumage when both have full black caps, but the crest length and bill shape remain reliable year-round.

### 6. Underpart Color
Breeding Elegant Terns have a delicate pinkish or rosy-pink wash on the breast and belly, a subtle but beautiful feature absent in Caspian. Caspian Terns are pure white below without any pink tinge. The pink bloom on Elegant is most visible in fresh breeding plumage and fades with wear.

## In the Field: Decision Rules
- Gull-sized, massive body → Caspian Tern
- Slender, medium-large tern → Elegant Tern
- Thick, straight blood-red bill → Caspian Tern
- Long, slender orange bill drooping at tip → Elegant Tern
- Extensively dark (blackish) underside of outer primaries in flight → Caspian Tern
- Pale underwing with dark only at primary tips → Elegant Tern
- Short, stiff bristly crest → Caspian Tern
- Long, shaggy drooping crest → Elegant Tern
- Rosy-pink breast tinge in breeding plumage → Elegant Tern
- Non-breeding: white forehead, black mask, long rear crest → Elegant Tern
- Non-breeding: dark streaky cap retained → Caspian Tern
- Pacific Coast California (esp. July–October) with long-billed slender terns → Elegant likely
- Large tern on any coast or inland lake → consider Caspian first

## Female / Immature / Non-breeding
Both sexes are identical within each species. Juvenile Caspian Terns have a scaly brown pattern on the upperparts, retained through their first fall. Juvenile Elegant Terns are also scaly above. In first-year birds, bill color may be less saturated (more yellowish-orange), but bill shape, primary pattern, and overall size remain reliable. Non-breeding adults of both species lose the full black cap; the differences described above in Section 5 apply.

## Voice
Caspian Tern gives a loud, harsh, crow-like *kraa-ah* or *ka-ah*, a low, rasping call that carries far and has a distinctly grating quality unlike any other tern. Elegant Tern has a higher-pitched, rolling, burry *krrr-ick* or *ker-EEK* call, more tern-like in pitch than Caspian's crow-like rasp. Voice is often useful when large terns are flying overhead.
