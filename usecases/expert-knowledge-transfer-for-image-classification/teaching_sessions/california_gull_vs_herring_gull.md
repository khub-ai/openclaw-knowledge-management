# California Gull vs Herring Gull: Identification Guide

**Source**: All About Birds (Cornell Lab of Ornithology); Sibley Guide to Birds
**Feature group**: bill markings + eye color + leg color
**Attribute similarity (CUB-200-2011)**: 0.9700

## Overview
Both are large, white-headed gulls with grey mantles, pink to yellowish legs, and yellow bills. California Gull is slightly smaller and more slender. In adult plumage the bill markings, eye color, and leg color reliably separate them. Immatures are notoriously difficult and are not the focus here.

## Key Identification Features

### 1. Bill markings (most reliable for adults)
Adult California Gull has both a red spot AND a black spot on the lower mandible (gonys). Adult Herring Gull has only a red (orange-red) gonydeal spot — no black mark. The California Gull's two-colored bill tip (red + black) is the single most reliable adult field mark.

### 2. Eye color
California Gull has a dark brown to dark hazel iris, giving it a dark-eyed, gentle expression. Herring Gull has a pale yellow iris. This is visible at moderate range and is one of the best supporting marks for adults.

### 3. Leg color
California Gull has greenish-yellow legs — neither the bright yellow of Ring-billed Gull nor the flesh-pink of Herring Gull, but a distinctive pale greenish or grey-green. Herring Gull's legs are flesh-pink. Leg color is reliable in adults but fades in photos taken in shade.

### 4. Overall size and head shape
California Gull is smaller and more slender than Herring Gull, with a rounder, more dove-like head and a more slender neck. Herring Gull has a heavier, blockier head and a more massive neck and chest. In mixed flocks the size difference is apparent.

### 5. Mantle shade
California Gull's mantle is slightly darker grey than Herring Gull's (though lighter than Lesser Black-backed). This is subtle and requires comparison.

## In the Field: Decision Rules
- If the bill shows both a red spot and a black mark near the tip of the lower mandible, it is a California Gull.
- If the bill shows only a red (orange-red) spot with no black, it is a Herring Gull.
- If the eye is dark brown, lean toward California Gull.
- If the eye is pale yellow, it is a Herring Gull.
- If the legs are greenish-yellow, it is a California Gull.
- If the legs are flesh-pink, it is a Herring Gull.
- If the bird appears smaller and rounder-headed than typical Herring Gulls nearby, consider California Gull.

## Female / Immature / Non-breeding
Immature gulls are extremely difficult. Both species go through 4 years of moult cycles. Focus only on adult or near-adult birds for this experiment. Non-breeding adults of both species show streaking on the head; bill markings and leg color remain reliable.

## Voice
Both give loud, yelping gull calls. Voice is not useful from photographs.

## Quick Reference
| Feature | California Gull | Herring Gull |
|---|---|---|
| Size | Medium-large (18–21 in) | Large (22–26 in) |
| Bill marking | Red + black spot | Red spot only |
| Eye | Dark brown | Pale yellow |
| Legs | Greenish-yellow | Flesh-pink |
| Head shape | Rounded, gentle | Heavy, blocky |
| Orbital ring | Red | Yellow |
