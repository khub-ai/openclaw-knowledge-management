# Northern Waterthrush vs Louisiana Waterthrush: Identification Guide

**Source**: All About Birds (Cornell Lab of Ornithology); Sibley Guide to Birds; Sibley Guides online
**Feature group**: Supercilium width/color, throat streaking, underpart pattern
**Attribute similarity (CUB-200-2011)**: ~0.81

## Overview
These two large, ground-walking wood-warblers (genus *Parkesia*) share near-identical brown-and-streaked plumage and an irresistible habit of pumping the tail up and down. The pair is considered among the most challenging in North American warbler identification. The key insight is to focus first on supercilium width and color, then confirm with throat streaking; habitat alone can often clinch a confident call before you even raise your binoculars.

## Key Identification Features

### 1. Supercilium (Eyebrow Stripe) — Most Discriminating Mark
Louisiana Waterthrush has a broad, strikingly white supercilium that typically flares or expands noticeably behind the eye, often appearing to "fan out" toward the rear of the head. It is clean white throughout, sometimes with a very faint buff tinge just above the lores. Northern Waterthrush has a narrower supercilium that is distinctly yellowish or buffy (rarely white), uniform in width from bill base to nape without a posterior flare. If the supercilium is broad, white, and fans out behind the eye → Louisiana. If it is narrow and yellowish-buff, essentially the same width front to back → Northern.

### 2. Throat Streaking
Louisiana Waterthrush shows a clean, unmarked white throat — the chin and upper throat are entirely free of streaks. This cleanness can appear almost startlingly white in the field. Northern Waterthrush has fine dark streaks or spots on the throat and chin; even in birds where they are subtle, close examination reveals speckling. An unstreaked white throat is diagnostic for Louisiana; a streaked throat eliminates it.

### 3. Underpart Streaking and Flank Color
Louisiana Waterthrush shows sparse, relatively coarse dark streaks on the breast that fade out on the lower belly, leaving the lower flanks largely white or very lightly marked. The flanks and undertail coverts often show a warm buffy or pinkish-buff wash that contrasts with the whiter chest. Northern Waterthrush has denser, finer streaking across the entire breast, often forming a more evenly streaked "necklace" effect, and the entire underpart background is yellowish-buff to whitish-yellow rather than pure white.

### 4. Bill Size and Shape
Louisiana Waterthrush has a noticeably longer, broader bill — proportionally larger-headed with a heavier bill (culmen length 10.2–12.2 mm) that creates a more substantial-looking face. Northern Waterthrush has a shorter, finer bill (9.0–10.7 mm), giving the head a more delicate appearance. This difference is most apparent in direct comparison but is a useful supporting character.

### 5. Leg Color
Louisiana Waterthrush legs are distinctly bubblegum pink (pale pinkish-flesh), noticeably brighter and paler than Northern. Northern Waterthrush legs are pinkish-brown to dull flesh, lacking the bright bubblegum quality. In good light, especially on clean-legged birds, this can be a useful supporting mark.

### 6. Habitat Context
Louisiana Waterthrush is tightly associated with clear, fast-flowing mountain streams and cold-water brooks in mature deciduous forest. It arrives on territory early (late March to April) and departs by late summer. Northern Waterthrush breeds primarily in boggy, still-water habitats — wooded swamps, lake edges with standing water, alder thickets — and migrates broadly through a wide range of wet habitats. If the bird is walking along a rushing stream in hilly deciduous woods in early spring → strongly favor Louisiana. If in a boggy shrub swamp or migrating through riparian scrub → Northern is more likely.

## In the Field: Decision Rules
- Broad white supercilium that flares behind the eye → Louisiana Waterthrush
- Narrow yellowish or buffy supercilium, uniform width → Northern Waterthrush
- Clean, unstreaked white throat → Louisiana Waterthrush
- Spotted or streaked throat/chin → Northern Waterthrush
- Buffy-pink flanks, lower belly nearly white, sparse streaking → Louisiana
- Densely streaked breast, yellowish-buff entire underparts → Northern
- Bubblegum-pink legs → Louisiana (supporting)
- Bird walking a rushing mountain stream in April → strongly consider Louisiana
- Bird in boggy thicket or lake-margin willows → strongly consider Northern

## Female / Immature / Non-breeding
Both sexes are identical in plumage within each species throughout the year. There is no seasonal plumage change in adults. Juvenile birds in their first fall can appear slightly duller with somewhat buffier underpart tones, but all the key features — supercilium shape, throat streaking pattern — remain intact. Fall migrants (especially Northern Waterthrush) are encountered widely; the yellowish tinge of the supercilium and streaked throat remain reliable in all plumages.

## Voice
Voice is highly useful and often the first clue. Louisiana Waterthrush gives a loud, ringing, three-part introductory slur followed by a descending tumble of notes: often described as *seeee-you, chip-chip-chip-deedle-ee*, strikingly loud and carrying for such a small bird, typically sung from a stream-side perch. Northern Waterthrush sings a series of emphatic, descending *sweet-sweet-sweet-swee-swee-chew-chew-chew* phrases, louder and more staccato, delivered at a rapid pace. The loud, musical quality of Louisiana's song and its stream-side habitat usually announce the species well before a visual is obtained. Both species give sharp *chink* call notes in the field.

## Quick Reference
| Feature | Northern Waterthrush | Louisiana Waterthrush |
|---|---|---|
| Supercilium width | Narrow, uniform | Broad, flares behind eye |
| Supercilium color | Yellowish-buff | Bright white |
| Throat | Streaked/spotted | Clean, unstreaked white |
| Underpart color | Yellowish-buff | White with buffy-pink flanks |
| Breast streaking | Dense, fine, uniform | Sparser, coarser, fades lower |
| Bill | Shorter, finer | Longer, broader |
| Legs | Dull pinkish-brown | Bubblegum pink |
