# Bronzed Cowbird vs Shiny Cowbird: Identification Guide

**Source**: All About Birds (Cornell Lab of Ornithology); Audubon Field Guide; Birds of the World
**Feature group**: Eye color, neck ruff, iridescence color, bill shape, female plumage
**Attribute similarity (CUB-200-2011)**: ~0.68

## Overview
Both are brood-parasitic New World blackbirds of similar size that can co-occur in the southern US and the Caribbean; Bronzed is primarily a southwestern US species while Shiny Cowbird is a Caribbean/South American species that has expanded into Florida and the Southeast. Both adult males appear as glossy black birds at a distance. The key insight is that the adult male Bronzed Cowbird looks like it is wearing a bronze cape and has glowing red eyes, while the adult male Shiny Cowbird is a sleeker, violet-glossed bird with a dark eye — eye color and neck ruff are the most decisive marks.

## Key Identification Features

### 1. Eye Color — Single Most Diagnostic Feature
Adult male Bronzed Cowbird has vivid, blood-red eyes — the iris is a striking crimson that is visible at moderate range in good light and is unlike any other common cowbird in North America. Adult female Bronzed Cowbird also shows red eyes (though slightly duller than males). Shiny Cowbird has dark eyes in all plumages — the iris appears blackish-brown and does not stand out against the dark face. If the eye appears red or orange-red → Bronzed Cowbird; if the eye is dark → Shiny Cowbird (and eliminate Brown-headed Cowbird similarly).

### 2. Neck Ruff and Head Shape
Male Bronzed Cowbird has a distinctive thick neck and enlarged, puffed nape region — a ruff of elongated feathers on the hindneck that gives the bird a hunched, heavy-shouldered, bull-necked silhouette. During courtship display, males inflate this ruff dramatically, hovering with the nape feathers fully puffed out around the female. Even at rest, the thick neck and high-shouldered profile is apparent. Shiny Cowbird has a slender, smooth-necked profile with no ruff; the neck flows cleanly into the shoulders, giving a sleeker, flatter-backed silhouette similar to a slim blackbird.

### 3. Gloss Color (Males)
Adult male Bronzed Cowbird has a primary gloss that is bronzy-green to purplish-blue on the wings and body — the bronzy tone on the back and head is what gives the species its name, appearing as a soft metallic sheen in angled sunlight. Adult male Shiny Cowbird is velvety purple-blue overall — the iridescence is a rich, uniform violet that in good light appears intense and saturated across the entire body and wings, without the bronzy-brown tones of Bronzed. At distance both look black, but the purple gloss of Shiny versus the bronze-green gloss of Bronzed is apparent in good light.

### 4. Bill Shape
Shiny Cowbird has a more slender, pointed bill — finer and more tapered toward the tip, similar in proportions to a slim blackbird bill. Bronzed Cowbird has a heavier, thicker-based bill with more bulk at the base; the bill appears more robust and less finely pointed than Shiny's. This difference is subtle but consistent and most apparent on comparison.

### 5. Female and Immature Plumage
Female Bronzed Cowbird in the eastern part of its range (Texas eastward) is blackish with a slight dull gloss; in the western part of the range (Arizona, California) she is grayish-brown overall. Female Bronzed retains red eyes. Female Shiny Cowbird is brown overall, warmer brown than most female cowbirds, with a pale supercilium and pale throat that give the face a slightly patterned appearance. Female Shiny lacks red eyes. The pale supercilium on female Shiny and the red eye on female Bronzed are the two marks to seek in females.

## In the Field: Decision Rules
- Red or orange-red iris → Bronzed Cowbird in any plumage
- Dark iris → Shiny Cowbird (male or female)
- Bull-necked, thick-necked profile with visible nape ruff → Bronzed Cowbird
- Slim, smooth-necked silhouette, no ruff → Shiny Cowbird
- Purple-blue iridescence uniformly saturated → Shiny Cowbird
- Bronze-green or purplish-blue gloss with bronzy tones → Bronzed Cowbird
- Female: blackish (eastern) or gray-brown (western) with red eye → Bronzed
- Female: warm brown with pale supercilium and dark eye → Shiny Cowbird
- Bird in Arizona, New Mexico, Texas ranch country → Bronzed Cowbird expected
- Bird in Florida or Caribbean → consider Shiny Cowbird (and check Brown-headed as well)

## Female / Immature / Non-breeding
Juvenile and immature Bronzed Cowbirds have dark eyes initially — immatures acquire the full red iris gradually through their first year. Juvenile Shiny Cowbirds are streaked brownish. When aging birds, the neck ruff and bill shape remain useful even in first-year birds, where eye color may not yet be definitive for Bronzed.

## Voice
Bronzed Cowbird gives an odd assortment of calls including wheezy, squeaky notes and high-pitched whistles. During courtship display, males produce thin, high-pitched *ee-eeee* sounds while hovering with neck ruff inflated. Shiny Cowbird gives a series of liquid, bubbling whistles and a distinctive gurgling song — often described as richer and more melodic than the harsh vocalizations of Bronzed. Voice can be helpful when the birds are heard calling, though range and visual marks should be primary.

## Quick Reference
| Feature | Bronzed Cowbird | Shiny Cowbird |
|---|---|---|
| Eye color | Vivid blood-red (all ages eventually) | Dark (blackish-brown) |
| Neck/nape | Thick neck, prominent ruff | Slim, smooth neck, no ruff |
| Male iridescence | Bronze-green to purplish-blue | Uniform velvety purple-blue |
| Bill | Heavier, thick-based | Slender, more finely pointed |
| Female plumage | Blackish or gray-brown | Warm brown with pale supercilium |
| Female eye | Red | Dark |
| Body size | Larger (avg ~65–70 g) | Smaller (avg ~40–50 g) |
| US range | SW US (AZ, NM, TX) to LA | FL, Caribbean; rare SE US |
