# Least Flycatcher vs Western Wood-Pewee: Identification Guide

**Source**: All About Birds (Cornell Lab of Ornithology); Sibley Guide to Birds; Audubon Field Guide; BirdWatching Magazine
**Feature group**: Eye ring presence, primary projection, body size, wing formula
**Attribute similarity (CUB-200-2011)**: ~0.71

## Overview
These two small, olive-gray flycatchers can appear superficially similar — both are gray-olive above, paler below, with two whitish wing bars — but they belong to different genera (*Empidonax* and *Contopus* respectively) and differ substantially in size, structure, and key facial features. The key insight is to check for three things immediately: eye ring presence (bold in Least, absent in Wood-Pewee), primary projection length (short in Least, long in Wood-Pewee), and overall breast pattern (clean in Least, vested in Wood-Pewee).

## Key Identification Features

### 1. Eye Ring — Most Instantly Obvious Mark
Least Flycatcher has a bold, complete white eye ring that stands out clearly against the gray-olive head — it is one of the crispest, most obvious eye rings of any Empidonax, visible immediately in the field. Western Wood-Pewee lacks an eye ring entirely; the face appears plain and uniformly colored without any white circle around the eye. The presence or absence of an eye ring is the single fastest field separator: bold white ring → Least Flycatcher; no ring → Wood-Pewee.

### 2. Primary Projection (Wing Tip Extension)
This structural feature is critical and visible on perched birds. Western Wood-Pewee has notably long primary projection — the primaries extend far beyond the tertials, creating a pointed, long-winged appearance on the folded wing. The tip of the longest primary visibly extends well past the tertial tips. Least Flycatcher has short primary projection; the primary tips barely extend past the tertials, giving the folded wing a blunt, rounded appearance. A short, blunt wingtip = Least Flycatcher; a long, pointed wingtip = Wood-Pewee.

### 3. Breast and Underpart Pattern ("Vested" Effect)
Western Wood-Pewee shows a characteristic "vested" appearance: the sides of the breast are smudgy gray, forming darker patches on each side that merge across the upper chest, contrasting with a paler whitish center of the belly. This creates a shadowed, vested look as if wearing an open jacket. Least Flycatcher has a pale gray wash across the breast without strong lateral darkening; the breast is more evenly washed without the vested contrast. Look for gray panels on the breast sides in Wood-Pewee.

### 4. Size
Western Wood-Pewee is noticeably larger at approximately 6.0–6.5 inches (about the size of a House Sparrow), compared to the Least Flycatcher at approximately 5.0–5.25 inches (among the smallest Empidonax flycatchers). The size difference is about 1.0–1.5 inches — significant enough to be apparent in the field, especially when directly compared or seen in familiar surroundings. Wood-Pewee has a more substantial, heavier-bodied appearance; Least looks petite.

### 5. Bill Size and Shape
Western Wood-Pewee has a proportionally longer, broader bill with a wide, flat base typical of the Contopus genus — the bill looks substantial relative to the head. Least Flycatcher has a shorter, more slender bill that appears smaller relative to its already small head. The bill base on Wood-Pewee is visibly broader, giving the head a wider, flatter-faced appearance when viewed from the front.

### 6. Wing Bars and Tertial Edging
Least Flycatcher has relatively bold, white wing bars and whitish tertial edgings that are crisply defined in fresh plumage. Western Wood-Pewee has duller, less contrasting wing bars that can appear faint, especially in worn summer birds; the tertial edgings are also less prominent. Wing bars alone do not separate the species reliably, but brighter, crisper wing bars support Least Flycatcher.

## In the Field: Decision Rules
- Bold white eye ring visible → Least Flycatcher
- No eye ring; plain face → Western Wood-Pewee
- Short primary projection; blunt, rounded folded wing → Least Flycatcher
- Long primary projection; pointed folded wing tip → Western Wood-Pewee
- Vested appearance: gray breast-side patches framing paler center → Western Wood-Pewee
- Even, pale gray breast wash without vested contrast → Least Flycatcher
- Small, sparrow-sized body (5 in) → Least Flycatcher
- Larger body, closer to 6.5 in → Western Wood-Pewee
- Bird in eastern deciduous or mixed forest, summer → Least Flycatcher expected
- Bird in open western woodland or conifer forest → Western Wood-Pewee expected

## Female / Immature / Non-breeding
Both sexes are identical within each species. Juveniles and first-fall birds can appear slightly buffier on the wing bars and tertial edges, but all key structural features (eye ring, primary projection, size) remain constant. Fall migrants can appear worn and washed out, reducing wing-bar contrast, but the presence or absence of the eye ring and primary projection length do not change with wear.

## Voice
Voice is the most reliable identifier if heard. Least Flycatcher gives a loud, emphatic, snappy *che-BEK* (accent on second syllable), typically repeated persistently from a perch — among the most recognized sounds of the eastern forest edge. Western Wood-Pewee gives a descending, burry, slightly harsh *pee-er* or *pee-ew* lacking the sharp, emphatic quality of Least Flycatcher's call; it also produces a short, rough *brrt*. The electric, snappy *che-BEK* of Least versus the lazy, descending *pee-er* of Wood-Pewee leaves no room for confusion when heard.
