# Herring Gull vs Ring-billed Gull: Identification Guide

**Source**: All About Birds (Cornell Lab of Ornithology); Sibley Guide to Birds
**Feature group**: size + bill ring + leg color
**Attribute similarity (CUB-200-2011)**: 0.9675

## Overview
Ring-billed and Herring Gulls are the two most commonly confused large gulls in North America. Ring-billed is smaller, slimmer, and reaches adult plumage faster (3 years vs. 4). In adult plumage the bill ring, leg color, and size reliably separate them. They frequently occur together in parking lots, beaches, and landfills.

## Key Identification Features

### 1. Bill pattern (diagnostic for adults)
Ring-billed Gull has a distinct black ring encircling the bill about one-third from the tip — this ring is complete, crisp, and visible at considerable range. Herring Gull lacks this ring; its bill is plain yellow with only a red-orange gonydeal spot. If you see a complete black ring on the bill, it is a Ring-billed Gull.

### 2. Leg color
Ring-billed Gull has yellow legs. Herring Gull has flesh-pink legs. Leg color is one of the most reliable adult field marks and visible from a distance.

### 3. Size and body proportions
Ring-billed Gull is noticeably smaller and slimmer — about the size of a large pigeon. Herring Gull is substantially larger, with a heavier body, thicker neck, and more massive head and bill. In mixed flocks the size difference is immediately apparent.

### 4. Eye and orbital ring
Both species have pale yellow eyes in adults. Ring-billed has a yellow orbital ring; Herring Gull has a yellow to orange-yellow orbital ring. The eye color difference alone is not useful; use the orbital ring only as a supporting feature.

### 5. Maturation rate
Ring-billed Gull reaches adult plumage in its third calendar year. Herring Gull takes four years. In any large gull flock, the smaller near-adult-plumage birds with black bill rings are Ring-billed; the larger birds in their second year still showing extensive brown mottling are typically Herrings.

### 6. Wingtip pattern
Adult Ring-billed has a black wingtip with white "mirrors" (spots) on the outer two primaries. Adult Herring Gull has a similar pattern but with larger mirrors and slightly more white in the wingtip. This difference is subtle.

## In the Field: Decision Rules
- If the bill has a complete black ring near the tip, it is a Ring-billed Gull.
- If the legs are yellow, it is a Ring-billed Gull.
- If the legs are flesh-pink, it is a Herring Gull.
- If the bill is plain yellow with only a red spot (no ring), it is a Herring Gull.
- If the bird is noticeably smaller and slimmer than the gulls around it, consider Ring-billed.
- If the bird is large and heavily built with a blocky head, lean toward Herring Gull.

## Female / Immature / Non-breeding
Immature birds are complex. Key shortcuts: immature Ring-billed has a pink-based bill with a sharp black tip (forming a proto-ring) by the first winter; immature Herring has an all-dark or dark-pink mottled bill. Non-breeding adults of both develop head streaking but retain their diagnostic leg color and bill pattern.

## Voice
Herring Gull gives the classic gull "laugh" — a loud, descending series of calls. Ring-billed gives a higher-pitched, more nasal version. Voice is secondary; not useful from photographs.

## Quick Reference
| Feature | Ring-billed Gull | Herring Gull |
|---|---|---|
| Size | Medium (17–21 in) | Large (22–26 in) |
| Bill | Black ring near tip | Red spot only, no ring |
| Legs | Yellow | Flesh-pink |
| Adult plumage | 3rd year | 4th year |
| Head shape | Rounded | Heavy, blocky |
