# Black-billed Cuckoo vs Yellow-billed Cuckoo: Identification Guide

**Source**: All About Birds (Cornell Lab of Ornithology); Sibley Guide to Birds; Audubon Field Guide
**Feature group**: Bill color, tail pattern, orbital ring color
**Attribute similarity (CUB-200-2011)**: ~0.72

## Overview
These two slender, long-tailed woodland cuckoos share nearly identical size, body shape, brown-above/white-below plumage, and secretive habits, making them one of the most confusing pairs in eastern North America. The key insight is a three-part gestalt check: bill color + tail spot size + orbital ring color, all of which are diagnostic when seen well.

## Key Identification Features

### 1. Bill Color
The most immediately obvious mark: Yellow-billed Cuckoo has a strongly bicolored bill with a black upper mandible and a distinctly yellow lower mandible, the yellow often extending forward past the midpoint. In sunlight the yellow flashes conspicuously. Black-billed Cuckoo, as the name advertises, shows an entirely dark, blackish bill on both mandibles. Any trace of yellow on the lower mandible eliminates Black-billed.

### 2. Tail Pattern (Undertail)
This is the single most reliable mark when the bill cannot be seen. Yellow-billed Cuckoo has large, high-contrast white spots occupying most of each black tail feather; from below, the tail looks like a boldly patterned black-and-white fan — the spots are the size of a thumbnail or larger. Black-billed Cuckoo has only narrow, whitish tips on the tail feathers; from below the tail appears largely gray with merely a thin pale fringe on each feather, lacking the bold white spots entirely.

### 3. Orbital Ring (Bare Skin Around Eye)
Examine the bare orbital ring when possible. Black-billed Cuckoo has a red or reddish-orange orbital ring, visible at moderate range in good light. Yellow-billed Cuckoo has a yellow to pale yellowish-brown orbital ring. This feature alone is diagnostic, though it requires closer views than bill or tail.

### 4. Rufous Wing Panel
Yellow-billed Cuckoo has a rufous (rusty-orange) patch on the primaries that is conspicuous in flight and sometimes visible on the folded wing as a warm brown tinge. Black-billed Cuckoo lacks this rufous panel; its primaries are plain brown, essentially matching the rest of the upperparts. In flight, a warm rufous flash in the wing points strongly to Yellow-billed.

### 5. Overall Size and Structure
Both species measure roughly 11–12 inches in length. Yellow-billed is on average slightly longer (11–12.5 in) with a proportionally longer bill that can look slightly decurved at the tip. Black-billed (10.5–12 in) has a slightly shorter, straighter bill. Differences are subtle and should not be used alone, but they reinforce other marks.

## In the Field: Decision Rules
- If the lower mandible is clearly yellow → Yellow-billed Cuckoo
- If the bill is entirely dark → Black-billed Cuckoo
- If the undertail shows large, bold white spots → Yellow-billed Cuckoo
- If the undertail appears grayish with only narrow pale fringes → Black-billed Cuckoo
- If rufous flashes in the wing in flight → Yellow-billed Cuckoo
- If the orbital ring is red → Black-billed Cuckoo
- If the orbital ring is yellow → Yellow-billed Cuckoo
- Plain brown wing panel with no rufous + narrow tail tips + dark bill → Black-billed Cuckoo

## Female / Immature / Non-breeding
Both sexes are identical in plumage in both species. Juvenile/first-basic birds are slightly duller above and may show buff tinging on the underparts, but retain all the key structural marks. Immature Black-billed may show slightly paler orbital ring, trending toward yellowish-buff, which can cause confusion; rely on bill color and tail pattern in all ages.

## Voice
Voice is often the best way to separate these two in dense cover. Black-billed Cuckoo gives a soft, repetitive series of hollow cu-cu-cu notes delivered at even pitch, running together in rhythmic groups of two to five; the tempo is rapid and monotone, without the "knocking" woody quality of Yellow-billed. Yellow-billed Cuckoo produces a descending series of hollow kuk-kuk-kuk notes that slow and deepen at the end into a resonant ca-ca-ca-cow, giving it a wooden, knocking character. When a cuckoo calls, the pace and terminal "slurring" of the Yellow-billed versus the even monotone of the Black-billed are usually sufficient for identification.

|---|---|---|
| Bill | Entirely dark/black | Yellow lower mandible, black upper |

| Orbital ring | Red to reddish-orange | Yellow to pale yellowish-brown |
